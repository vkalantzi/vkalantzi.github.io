Here I discuss some of the applications of numerical linear algebra, mostly eigenvalue problems, in real-world problems. This discussion is by no means complete, or exhaustive; rather it is based on personal notes taken while doing research on the origins of large-scale numerical linear algebra computations.


Electronic structure calculations

Eigenvalue problems hold an important role in the study of the electronic structure of many-body systems. Exploiting the Density Functional Theory (DFT) framework, the solution of the all-electron Schr $\ddot{o}$dinger equation is replaced by a one-electron Schr $\ddot{o}$dinger equation with an effective potential which leads to a nonlinear eigenvalue problem, known as the Kohn-Sham equation: \begin{equation} \label{eq:kseq} - \left[\frac{\nabla^2}{2} + V_{ion}(r) + V_H(\rho(r), r) + V_{XC}(\rho(r), r)\right] \Psi_i(r) = E_i \Psi_i(r) \end{equation} where $\Psi_i(r)$ is a wave function and $E_i$ is a Kohn-Sham eigenvalue. The ionic potential $V_{ion}$ reflects contributions from the core and depends on the position $r$ only. Both the Hartree and the Exchange-Correlation potentials depend on the charge density: \begin{equation}\label{eq:rho} \rho(r) = 2 \sum_{i=1}^{n_{occ}} |\Psi_i(r)|^2, \end{equation} where $n_{occ}$ is the number of occupied states (for most systems of interest this is half the number of valence electrons). Since the total potential $V_{total} = V_{ion}+V_H + V_{XC}$ depends on $\rho(r)$ which itself depends on eigenfunctions of the Hamiltonian, Equation (\ref{eq:kseq}) can be viewed as a nonlinear eigenvalue problem or a nonlinear eigenvector problem. The Hatree potential $V_H$ is obtained from $\rho$ by solving the Poisson equation $ \nabla^2 V_H(r) = -4 \pi \rho(r)$ with appropriate boundary conditions. The Exchange-Correlation term $V_{XC}$ is the key to the DFT approach and it captures the effects of reducing the problem from many particles to a one-electron problem, i.e., from replacing wavefunctions with many coordinates into ones that depend solely on space location $r$.

Self-consistent iterations for solving the Kohn-Sham equation start with an initial guess of the charge density $\rho(r)$, from which a guess for $V_{total}$ is computed. Then (\ref{eq:kseq}) is solved for $\Psi_i(r)$'s and a new $\rho(r)$ is obtained from (\ref{eq:rho}) and the potentials are updated. Then (\ref{eq:kseq}) is solved again for a new $\rho$ obtained from the new $\Psi_i(r)$'s, and the process is repeated until the total potential has converged. A typical electronic structure calculation with many atoms requires the calculation of a large number of eigenvalues, specifically the $n_{occ}$ leftmost ones. In addition, calculations based on Time-Dependent Density Functional Theory, require a substantial number of unoccupied states, states beyond the Fermi level, in addition to the occupied ones. Thus, it is not uncommon to see eigenvalue problems in the size of millions where (tens) of thousands of eigenvalues may be needed.

Finite element modal and frequency response analysis

Engineering structures have a set of natural frequencies (the frequency at which a structure tends to oscillate in the absence of any external force). If a structure is excited by an external force whose frequency is close to a natural frequency, the structure starts oscillating and the amplitude of the vibration increases manyfold (i.e., resonance takes place). Frequency response analysis describes how a structure responds to a prescribe load over a range of frequencies, and allows to determine its resonance behavior. Ideally, forced and natural frequencies should not be close (see here for an example of resonance).

Assuming a closed domain without damping, the frequency response function is often modeled as the solution of the following linear system: \begin{equation} K-\omega^2 M = f, \label{modan} \end{equation} where $K$ and $M$ are symmetric matrices known as the stiffness and mass matrix, respectively. The right-hand side $f$ represents the force applied to the structure. Variable $\omega$ represents the frequency of interest, and, typically, takes values within a frequency band $\Omega=[\omega_l,\omega_u]$. The standard approach of solving equation (\ref{modan}) for all different frequency instances becomes computationally intractable for modern Finite Element models since the size of the stiffness and mass matrices can easily be in the order of millions. An alternative is to project equation (\ref{modan}) on a low-dimensional subspace, drastically reducing the dimension of the problem, and compute the frequency response of the projected structure for as many frequency instances as necessary, instead. The most commonly used choice is to form the aforementioned low-dimensional subspace by the eigenvectors of the matrix pancil $(K,M)$ which correspond to eigenvalues which magnitude is located within a certain region on the real axis. The latter leads to the necessity to partially solve an eigenvalue problem of the form $Kx=\lambda M x$. Matrix $M$ is positive-definite, while the accuracy sought for the eigenpairs is of the order of the discretization error of the FE model.
